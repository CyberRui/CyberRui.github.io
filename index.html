What the questions do we want to answer?

In this work, we want to expolre how we can do the stratified micro-randomized trial design in causal inference. For example, we create one strategy to portable device to measure the stress level of user and give the notification. As for the randomized gauge, we use the stratifed strategy.

What (observed or unobserved) random variables are needed to fully model the problem?

Availability:The indicator $I_t = 1$ means that the participant is available at decision time $t$ and $I_t = 0$ otherwise.
Stratification variable:The stratification varibale is $X_t$. In our Sense2Stop, there are two srata;$X_t=1$ indicates $t$ is within an episode which, at the peak of this episode,the participant was classified as stressed and $X_t=0$ otherwise. $X_t$ is only obseved in real time if $t$ is the minute following the peak and this is also called availablility of participant above.
Treatment: $A_t$ is a binary random variable means that if $A_t = 1$, denote the participant is chosen to receive a reminder to parctice stress reduction exercises at available time and $A_t = 0$ otherwise.
Proximal response:Usually treatment are designed to have a proximal, near-term effect on a response variable. The proximal response, denoted here by $Y_{t,\Delta}$, is the function of the participant's data within a sunsequent window of length $\Delta$. Here in the paper, the proximal outcome is the fraction of time classified as stressed over the subsequent $\Delta = 60$ minutes. As the previous setting of this paper, the real time stress calssification is made at the peak of an episode and after the classification is made, the prior minutes in the same episode recieve the same classfication. Here this setting give us the relationship between $X_t$ and $Y_{t,\Delta}$:
$$Y_{t,\Delta} = \Delta^{-1}\sum_{s=1}^{\Delta}X_{t+s}=1$$

What assumptions are you making for identification, and why?

The The following three assumptions are used to express the causal treatment effect, $Œ≤(t; x)$, in terms of the observable data.
$\textbf{Assumption 4.1}$ We assume consistency, positivity, and sequential ignorability: Consistensy: For each $t\leq T + \Delta,\{ X_t(\overline{A}_{t-1}),I_t(\overline{A}_{t-1})\} = \{X_t.I_t\}.$ That is, the observed values equal the corresponding potential outcomes.
Positivity: if the joint density $\{ H_t = h, A_t = a\}$ is greater than zero, then $pr(A_t = a_t|H_t = h_t)>0.$
Sequential ignorability: for each $t\leq T$, the potential outcomes, $\{X_2(a_1),I_2(a_1),...,X_{T+\Delta}(\overline{a}_{T+\Delta - 1})\}_{\overline{a}_{T+\Delta - 1}}\in \{ 0,1\}^{T+\Delta-1},$ are independent of $A_t$ conditional on the history $H_t$.
Reason: Sequential ignorability and, assuming all of the randomization probabilities are bounded away from 0 and 1, positivity, are guaranteed for an sMRT by design. Consistensy is a necessary assumption for linking the potential outcomes as defined here to the data. When an individual's outcomes may be influenced by the treatments provided to other individuals, consistency may not hold. In such instances, a group-based conceptualization of potential outcomes is used. In particualr, if the mobile intervention includes treatments that aim to produce social ties between participants, then consistency as stated above will not hold. For simplicity, we do not consider such mobile intervention here.

Firstly,we use $L_2$ projections and provide the test statistics to get the sample size formula to get sufficient power to detect alternatives to the null hypothesis. Secondly, we need to determin the sample size N, the step is following:
1.desired type 1 and type 2 error rates.
2.targeted alternatice $\beta(t;x),$
3.seleted probabilities ${p_t(1|x)}$,
4.seleted "control variables" $g_t(H_t)$,
5.the randomization formula used to determine $p_t(1|h)$ given a history h, and
6.a generative model for $H_{t=1,...,T}$

How exactly does data factor into estimation?

In the following, we take the general three-step procedure and walk through how to adapt it to the specifics of the Sense2Stop study and form the sample size calculator. Recall the last step involves a variety of simulations to assess robustness to the assumption underlying the generative model;
As noted previously, Sense2Stop is a 10 day study; the first day is the ‚Äúquit day‚Äù, the day the participant quits smoking. Recall that participants wear the AutoSense sensor suite [Ertin et al., 2011] which provides a variety of physiological data streams. During the conduct of the study, the stratification variable, Xt is constructed online. Xt = 1 if at minute t there is ‚Äúsufficient evidence‚Äù that the participant is in a stress episode; otherwise Xt = 0. That is, until there is sufficient evidence whether that the participant is in a stress episode, Xt remains unknown. Further information on these episodes follows. First, every minute, a support vector machine (SVM) algorithm is applied to a number of ECG and respiration features constructed from the prior one minute stream of sensor data. The output of the SVM is then transformed to obtain a stress ‚Äúlikelihood‚Äù in (0, 1); see Hovsepian et al. [2015] for details. This output (in (0, 1)) across the minute intervals is further smoothed to obtain a smoother stress likelihood time series. Next, a Moving Average Convergence Divergence approach is used to identify minutes at which the trend in the stress likelihood is going up and when it is going down; see Sarker et al. [2016] for details. The beginning of an episode is marked by the start of a positive-trend interval in the stress ‚Äúlikelihood.‚Äù Recall the peak of an episode is the end of a positive-trend interval followed by the start of a negative-trend interval. If the area under the curve from the beginning of the episode to the minute that the peak of the episode is detected exceeds a threshold, then at this time the individual is classified as stressed for all minutes t in the episode (i.e., Xt = 1). At all other times, the participant belongs to the not classified as stressed strata (i.e., Xt = 0). The threshold is based on prior data from lab experiments. and was evaluated on independent test datasets (from both lab and field) in terms of the F1 score (a combination of sensitivity and specificity ) for use in detecting physiological stress.

If (1) holds and (2) does not, it may be that prior scientific knowledge can suggest potential deviations that are difficult to estimate given the small size of prior data (scenario B). If (1) holds and (2) does not, alternatively, it may be that we can account for additional variation via fitting more complex models to the Minnesota dataset (scenario C).
